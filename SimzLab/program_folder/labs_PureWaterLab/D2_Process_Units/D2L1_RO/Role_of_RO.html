Role of RO in UPW

An important function of RO in UPW production is desalination or the removal of various ionic species.  The efficiency of RO treatment is generally noted as the rejection ratio of sodium chloride.  When evaluating total salt rejection, removal of polyhydric ions is higher than that of monohydric ions.  Free carbonic acid is not removed.  The removal of silica and ammonia is relatively low in comparison with other pure water processes; however, special synthetic polymer composite membranes have recently been developed that are capable of efficient silica removal. Since synthetic polymer composite membranes have minimal charge, ion performance removal may vary considerably with the feed water pH.

Reverse osmosis units are also designed for removal of total organic carbon (TOC) from UPW.  TOC removal efficiencies differ according to the quality and texture of the membrane.  Generally, cellulose acetate-based membranes have a low rate of removal of low molecular weight organics (200-300 Daltons).  For example, alcohols such as methanol, propanol, and acetone are not removed.  Compared to cellulose acetate membranes with the same desalination abilities, synthetic polymer membranes are very good at removing organic matter.  Removal of organics varies with pH.  For example, removal of carboxylic acid is influenced by pH due to the dissociation of organic matter.  Generally, removal of TOC is 70%-90% in upstream RO and 40%-70% in downstream RO. The difference in organic rejection can be explained by the fact that the upstream RO works extremely well on the larger organics, mainly due to physical rejection.  However, the smaller organics tend to pass through both RO units, resulting in a lower TOC removal efficiency in the second unit.  RO is also effective in removal of fine particles, microorganisms, and colloidal substances.
