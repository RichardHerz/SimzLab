History

The history of the discovery and development of osmosis and reverse osmosis is discussed in detail in literature.  In Water Quality and Treatment (1990), Fredrick Pontius provides details on how the French physicist Nollet discovered the phenomenon of osmosis in 1748.  Nollet observed that water would spontaneously diffuse through a pig bladder into an alcohol solution.   In 1867, German chemist Traube began performing experiments with artificial membranes.  Few developments were made until the 1950s when Reid and Breton developed cellulose acetate membranes.  These membranes exhibited poor flux due to excessive thickness.  Later, Loeb and Sourirajan developed a cellulose acetate membrane at UCLA which had high flux and salt removal of 95%.  Many improvements have been made since the early 1970s, including the creation of specialized membranes for different applications.  Research and development of new membrane technology continues today, and there are now at least ten major manufacturers worldwide, including General Electric, Toray, and Pall Corporation.  The materials presented below provide only a brief introduction of the subjects.  Additional information can be found at www.perfectlife.com, www.watecinc.com, and www.engineer.ucla.edu/history/osmosis.html.
