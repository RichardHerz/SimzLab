History

While the mechanisms behind ion exchange are a relatively recent discovery, the use of certain materials to purify drinking water has been used as far back as ancient Greece. In the Bible, for example, the book of Exodus (15:22-25) describes how Moses miraculously made bitter water sweet by throwing a piece of wood in it.  Considered a miracle to the ancients, the techniques of water purification by ion exchange remained mostly an art as opposed to science until the mid 19th century.  Then, in 1848, English agricultural chemist H.S. Thompson reported to J. Thomas Way that some soils released lime, (CaCO3) after treatment with ammonium carbonate ((NH4)2CO3) (Kunin, 1958).  Way followed up Thompson’s observations by conducting several years of extensive study (regarded as the first scientific investigation of ion exchange phenomena), and made several important conclusions:

·         Ions are exchanged in equivalent quantities

·         Certain ions are more readily exchanged than others

·         The extent of the exchange depends on concentration

Subsequent experiments in 1876 involving the interconversion of the minerals leucite (K2O·Al2O3·4SiO2) and analcite (Na2O·Al2O3·4SiO3·2H2O) with aqueous salt solutions demonstrated the reversibility of ion exchange processes (Kunin, 1958).  Synthetic resins were not synthesized until 1935 when B.A. Adams and E. Leighton Holmes made a polymer derived from tannic acid that could change the color of a solution of ferric chloride (FeCl3) (Adams and Holmes, 1935).  Almost immediately, scientists realized that synthetically synthesized resins could be tailored to meet different specifications.
