Operation

Mixing and Traps

Proper mixing and even distribution of UV light on incoming water is essential to maintain efficient UV disinfection. Suspended particles and solid objects in the water modify exposure time by light shadowing and should be removed prior to UV treatment. Also, traps and dead zones should be avoided so that new water continuously flows through and treated water flows out of the reaction chamber. Water turbidity, or how cloudy the water is, should be kept to a minimum for inlet flow. In UPW systems, most of the conditions of the inlet water should be satisfactory for UV disinfection based on outlets from previous UPW systems.

 

Metals and Other UV Absorption

In addition to avoiding suspended particles in the inlet water, other compounds and factors can disrupt UV disinfection. Trace amounts of magnesium or iron in the quartz sleeve will absorb UV 185 nm (DeGenova, 2001). These trace amounts can be applied to low-pressure lamps when only 254 nm is required. But the synthetic pure quartz sleeve discussed earlier is necessary for medium-pressure lamps or when using unfiltered UV radiation that includes 185nm.

 

The AWWA reports that some proteins, humic material, and iron compounds in the water can shield microorganisms by absorbing UV radiation. However, monitoring and adjusting the UV light intensity inside the reactor is the simplest method of preventing these complications (1990). In UPW application, inlet water should be clear and free of light absorbing particles since it arrives from other UPW processes upstream, but light intensity monitoring is still recommended.

 

The hardness and pH of water can also indirectly hinder UV light effectiveness. Both properties affect the solubility of metals in water, which increases the chance for more UV absorbing metals. Also, water hardness can lead to the precipitation of carbonates in the reactor, increasing the occurrence of maintenance and cleaning and decreasing light intensity (USEPA, 1999). Preceding processes in UPW systems generally control the hardness and pH of inlet stream water, so these concerns are minimal.

 

Lamp Cleaning and Maintenance

Regular cleaning of UV lamps is necessary to prevent fouling on the lamps and to maintain efficient light intensity. Insufficient cleaning of the reactor and lamp is the major cause of an ineffective UV system. Cleaning is usually done by mechanical wiping, ultrasonic, or chemical mechanisms. Chemical cleaning is commonly done with citric acid, but can also be done with mild vinegar solutions or sodium hydrosulfite (USEPA, 1999). The type of cleaning mechanism and the frequency of cleaning varies for different UV systems based on their specific O&M.

 

The EPA reports an average UV lamp life of 8,760 to 14,000 working hours and the quartz sleeve life from 5 to 8 years. Lamps are typically replaced after 12,000 working hours (500 days) and quartz sleeves after 5 years (USEPA, 1999).

 

Temperature

The temperature of the water in low-pressure UV lamps affects light output intensity. Without a quartz sleeve, the optimum operating temperature for a UV lamp is about 40 ºC. At 20 ºC the output drops to about 50% and at 0 ºC the output drops to about 10%. With a quartz sleeve as a boundary between the lamp and water, the temperature effects are reduced. The optimum water temperature is about 22 ºC and at 0 ºC the light output drops to about 80% (Bolton, 2004).

 

For high-output or medium-pressure lamps, the effect of water temperature is minimal. This flexibility supports the versatile applications of UV treatment.

 

Secondary Disinfectant

The UV treatment processes do not produce a beneficial residual, such as free-chlorine, to protect the treated water for distribution, so a secondary disinfectant is usually required. Common disinfectants include chloramines and/or chlorine (AWWA, 1990).  However, in a UPW system, this secondary disinfectant is not desired since it would further contaminate the ultra pure water. Also, distribution of the water occurs rapidly, and other methods of protection are used and implemented in other UPW processes.

 

Dark Repair

Some organisms have the capability to reverse and repair the effects of UV DNA destruction in the absence of light through a phenomenon called “dark repair,” also known as photo reactivation. The causes of this repair mechanism are still unknown, but dark repair effects should be taken into consideration when transporting water after UV treatment. Most cases of dark repair have been reported in distribution pipes for treated water. In UPW systems, the effects of this phenomenon are minimal since the UV treated water is sent promptly to the next process with little time spent not being treated.

 

Advantages & Safety

UV radiation is a safe and effective method of water disinfection. Most viruses, bacteria, spores, and cysts can be lethally inactivated using UV disinfection. The required residence time is short, often only 20-30 seconds, so more water can be processed at a higher flow rate. Generally, UV systems require less space and less cost to operate than other methods of water treatment. UV disinfection operates more efficiently when functioning together with other UPW systems.

 

There are minimal safety considerations for UV disinfection compared to other UPW systems. UV radiation is simply light waves, unrelated to nuclear radiation; by itself the light does not produce harmful environmental chemical byproducts. Additionally, no added chemicals are needed for the physical mechanism of the UV reaction. There are no toxic chemicals that need to be stored or handled. Even high-intensity UV systems have a low cost energy requirement and require very simple maintenance in relation to the other UPW systems. The absence of dangerous chemicals to monitor and the high compatibility with other disinfection treatments make UV radiation a very effective system for UPW application.

 

The largest safety concern in UV disinfection is exposure to UV light produced. Although the UV lamp is fully enclosed for a majority of usage, special care must be taken when handling, i.e. physical cleaning. Since UV-C damages all DNA, including human cells, exposed skin should be covered. Also, looking directly into the light can cause severe damage to the eyes. Contact with the lamp while it is powered should be avoided. Under normal operating conditions, electrical hazards and UV radiation concerns are minimal.
