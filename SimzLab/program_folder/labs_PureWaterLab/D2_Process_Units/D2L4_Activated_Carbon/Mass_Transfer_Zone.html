  Mass Transfer Zone  
     
     
            In packed-bed contactor due to the continuous adsorption, the flow of contaminated water creates a wave front as it flow through the activated carbon bed.  This wave front is better known as the mass transfer zone (MTZ).  This is where active adsorption happens in a packed-bed reactor.  Within the MTZ, the degree of saturation with adsorbate varies from 100 percent to effectively zero.  As the activated carbon reaches its equilibrium capacity or in other words becomes exhausted, the MTZ will travel further through the carbon bed.  When the MTZ moves across the carbon bed it leaves behind a section of carbon bed, which is completely exhausted, and in front of the leading edge of the MTZ lays fresh carbon.  The MTZ will continue to travel through the activated carbon bed until the carbon bed hits its breakthrough point.  The breakthrough point is defined when a preset concentration emerges in the effluent, and the effluent quality no longer meets predetermined treatment objectives.  When breakthrough occurs the activated carbon will need to be replaced to keep excessive concentrations of impurities out of the effluent.   
     
            An important design consideration is the length of the MTZ.  Minimizing the length of the MTZ allows for more of the capacity of the activated carbon to be utilized for any predetermined treatment objective.  The length of the MTZ is a function of the influent flow rate and the rate of adsorption.  This means that any parameter that changes the rate of adsorption will also change the length of the MTZ to some extent.  Below is a convenient equation to calculate the length of the MTZ.
