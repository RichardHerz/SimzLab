History  
   
The use of carbon for adsorption dates back as early as 1550 B.C.  The first uses for porous carbon was for medical purposes.  In an ancient Egyptian papyrus, it was described that charcoal was used to adsorb odors from putrid wounds and also in the intestinal tract (History of Carbon and Carbon Materials, 1995-2006).  Later, Hippocrates, an ancient Greek physician who has been regarded as “the father of medicine”, also described the uses of porous carbon for mainly medicinal purposes.  Wood chars were used to treat various different ailments such as: epilepsy, chlorosis, and anthrax (History of Carbon and Carbon Materials, 1995-2006).

In 1773, Karl Wilhelm Scheele was the first known man to quantify the adsorption forces of porous carbon.  This was done by measuring the volumes of various gases that could be adsorbed by the varying sources of carbon.  A little more than a decade later, in 1785, J.T. Lowitz using established knowledge of the adsorption power of porous carbon on odor vapors applied this to aqueous solutions (Hassler, 1974).  Charcoal was used to decolorize these aqueous solutions.  This was the first known account of using charcoal to adsorb compounds in the liquid phase.  Building from the works of its precursors, an English sugar refinery in 1794 was successfully able to use wood char to decolorize the sugar syrups that were being produced.  By 1808, all the sugar refineries located in Europe were now using charcoal as a decolorizing agent and by 1815, most of the refineries switched over to the newly developed bone char as the decolorizing agent (History of Carbon and Carbon Materials, 1995-2006).  The bone char enhanced the capacity of the decolorizing agent. 
At the turn of the century, a major advancement in the progression of modern commercial activated carbon occurred.  Von Ostrejko patented, in 1900 and 1901, two separate processes in which porous carbon was given greater adsorbing power.  The first of the two processes incorporated adding metallic chlorides with the carbonaceous materials before the carbonization stage.  The other patent involved gentle selective oxidation of the charred carbonaceous material with carbon dioxide or steam at elevated temperatures.  These processes had the interest of the people because of the great potential activated carbon meant to the sugar cane industry (Hassler, 1974).

It was not until the introduction of chemical warfare in World War I that the world saw the need for large-scale production of activated carbon.  It was known from previous experiments that certain materials could be used to adsorb the toxic vapors created from the chemical weapons used.  Activated carbon was used in its granulated form, because of the adequate adsorption power and the relatively low resistance to air flow through the canister of the gas masks that were developed.  The first set of gas masks used activated wood chips through activation with zinc chloride (History of Carbon and Carbon Materials, 1995-2006).  Later research during the war concluded that coconut shells, as the activated carbon source, gave the best results in the gas mask canisters.  The developments that occurred during wartime resulted in a post war expansion in the commercial production of activated carbon, with emphasis on the production of activated carbon from coconut and almond shells.  This post war expansion has lead to the widespread use of activated carbon as a filter media in many daily processes.
