  Properties  
     
  Iodine Number:  Iodine is the most fundamental parameter used to characterize performance and is widely used as an adsorbate standard to the adsorption capacity.  It reveals the ability to adsorb small molecules; a higher number indicates a higher degree of activation.  Because Iodine has a relatively low molecular weight, the Iodine number appropriately measures the adsorption capacity of the micropore (0-20 Å) structure of the activated carbon.  The specific definition of Iodine number is the milligrams of Iodine adsorbed by one gram of carbon.  This value can also approximate the inner surface area of the carbon particle in m2/g.  
     
  Molasses Number / Decolorizing Index:  The value of the molasses number is used for measuring the decolorizing performance of the carbon sample.  The color pigments that are within molasses are rather large molecules and cannot penetrate into the micropore structure of the carbon sample.  Therefore, the molasses number is also an approximation of the adsorption capacity of the carbon sample in the macropore (>500 Å).  
     
  Methylene Blue:  The value of the Methylene blue parameter is very similar to that of the Iodine number.  The Methylene blue parameter measures the adsorption capacity of medium sized particles.  It differs from Iodine Number in that the Methylene Blue parameter measures the adsorption capacity of the mesopore structure (20-500 Å).  The greater the value the Methylene blue parameter, the better the carbon sample will adsorb particles in the mesopore region of its intricate pore structure.  
     
  Surface Area:  The total surface area of the carbon sample is a very important parameter that classifies the carbon sample overall activity level or adsorption capacity.  The total surface area of the carbon sample varies with the type of carbonaceous material used and the degree of activation that the carbonaceous undergoes.  
     
  Apparent Density: When talking about activated carbon, especially granular activated carbon, a regular density would not describe anything useful.  It would basically describe a solid without a high degree of surface area and, in turn, would describe a solid with essentially no apparent adsorptive capacity.  A much more sensible method for describing the density of a carbon sample is the apparent density.  The apparent density is the mass of the nonstratified dry activated carbon per unit volume of activated carbon.  A higher apparent density means great volume activity.  Therefore, the apparent density will be a considerably reduced value from the standard density, because of the intricate network of pores a sample of activated carbon possess.  
     
  Particle Size/Mesh Size:  The particle size of a sample of activated carbon is very useful in determining design parameters for a given activated carbon filtration system.  Some of the design considerations that need to be taken into account when deciding on the mesh size of the carbon used is: pressure drop in bed, adsorption capacity, rate of backwash stream, concentration of contaminants in the influent stream.  A smaller mesh size of carbon will increase the rate of adsorption and also increase the pressure drop across the fluidized bed.  
     
  Hardness/Abrasion Number: The abrasion number is usually applicable in systems where there is a frequent, continuous use of granular carbon.  The Hardness/Abrasion Number indicates the resistance to attrition and its physical integrity.  It is an important indicator of activated carbon to maintain its physical integrity and withstand frictional forces; a high relative value of hardness correlates with a reduced amount of carbon loss.  Hardness becomes very important in the flow in fluidized beds.  Carbon with a low abrasion number may become brittle and fall apart under the pressure of the influent stream.  
     
  Ash Content: Ash is the mineral residue that is left on the surface of a carbon sample after activation or regeneration.  The overall content of the ash will affectedly reduce the activity level of the carbon sample.  Furthermore, high ash content will reduce the efficiency of regeneration of the exhausted carbon.  Depending on the mineral residues present on the carbon, pretreatment of the carbon may reduce the ash content.  In some cases, the natural ash found on the carbon sample will not affect the nature of the adsorption process.  
     
  Tannin: The tannin describes the ability of the activated carbon with macropores and measpores to adsorb a mixture of large and medium size molecules.  The tannin is reported in parts per million.  
     
  Dechlorination: Dechlorination measures the chlorine efficiency removal of the activated carbon.  The dechlorination half-value length is the depth of carbon required to reduce the chlorine level of a flowing stream from 5 ppm to 3.5 ppm. A lower half-value length indicates superior performance.
