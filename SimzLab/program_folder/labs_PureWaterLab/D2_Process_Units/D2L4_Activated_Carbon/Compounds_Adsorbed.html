With all water treatment processes, activated carbon also has its limitations.  These limitations arise not only from certain compounds that are for all measurable accounts impervious to perification via activated carbon, but also the conditions of the influent.  As mentioned before, activated carbon can adsorb almost all organic compounds to some degree.  The exception to this rule is organic compounds that have low molecular weights.  Compounds that have a low molecular weight can easily navigate the intricate pore network without binding to the surface by the relatively minute adsorption forces.  Another general limitation created by some organic compounds is those compounds that pose high polarity.  As a general rule, similar materials tend to associate with one another.  This is why non-polar compounds tend to associate with the activated carbon surface, which is also non-polar, than staying dissolved in the polar solution of water.  Even if an influent stream meets all the general rules for organic compounds, they can still pose limitations to the activated carbon.  These limitations arise when the influent either has a high concentration of organic materials or high concentration of suspended solids.  When there is a high concentration of organic material, the activated carbon will usually approach exhaustion very rapidly and would not be economically sound to be used as the purification method.  When there is a high concentration of suspended solids, they might cause fouling of the carbon and require frequent backwashing.
