Manufacture  
          The first step in the manufacture of activated carbon is the selection of the carbonaceous material.  The raw carbonaceous material plays an important role in the performance and characteristics that the activated carbon will possess at the completion of the manufacturing process.  There is a very wide spectrum of the types of raw materials that can be used; the common trait amongst them is all need to have high carbon content.

            The production of activated carbon entails two main processes: carbonization and activation.  Before the selected material can go through these processes, there are a few preliminary steps.  Incorporated in these initial steps are the methods to bring the starting materials all to a uniform predetermined size.  The production techniques used to get the material to a uniform size and shape will also affect the roughness and the hardness of the carbon sample.  For example, pelletization and crushing result in a final carbon sample with a more smooth roughness, whereas extrusion or agglomeration results in a carbon surface that has more roughness to it.  These sizing processes can lead to an end product in the size range of 1-10 mm.  Studies have shown that varying types of sizing processes that can be carried out affect the size, shape, roughness, and hardness, but also the macropore system of the carbon is changed.  
     
            The macropore system of the carbon sample is mostly changed through the carbonization step.  Carbonization, which is sometimes called pyrolysis or charring, incorporates exposing the starting carbon to elevated temperature in the absence of air.  The elevated temperature are usually in the magnitude of 600ºC, but is not standardized because the differences of temperature allow for differences in desired properties of the carbon sample.  For the duration of the carbonization process, volatile products contained in the structure of the starting carbon are removed thus leaving a pore system in their absence.  The carbonization products also leave behind residues called coke or char.  One can achieve a greater level of the macropore system using dehydrating additives in the carbonization process.  The dehydrating additives most commonly used are zinc chloride and phosphoric acid.  
     
            The activation process that follows carbonization is where the carbon sample receives their micropore system.  Activation consists of applying oxidizing agents, such as carbon dioxide and oxygen, to the chars that were produced in the carbonization step.  The char and the oxidizing agents are again subjected to elevated temperatures up to approximately 900oC.  Though the course of the activation step, the oxidizing agents attack sections of the char.  In the absence of the sections of char, micro pores are developed.  The process of activation has a heightened effect in creating an activated carbon sample with superior adsorption qualities exhibited from the micropore structure.
