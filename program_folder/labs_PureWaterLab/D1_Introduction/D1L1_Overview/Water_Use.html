Water Use Overview

FAST FAX

Water Comprises 55% to 60% of our body mass (Timberlake, 2005).

Water is all around us.  Often when we think of water, we think of lakes, rivers and oceans, rain and snow.  However it is also in the air (as humidity) which forms dew on cool mornings or ice on your windshield in the winter.  The humidity in the air is also responsible for the water droplets that form on the outside of your iced tea glass on a warm summer day. 

Water is an inorganic compound with the molecular formula H2O.  It has a molecular weight of 18 grams/mole.  Additional tidbits about water can be found in the “Fast Fax” boxes located throughout this section. 

FAST FAX

Approximately 71% of the earth's surface is covered by water (Tomczak, 1999).

Water is the critical component of water sports, personal hygiene, agriculture and manufacturing processes.  An enormous quantity of fresh and salt water is used each day throughout the world.  For example, in 1990, the average daily water usage in the United States was approximately 339,000 million gallons.  That is 339,000,000,000 gallons or 339 BILLION gallons of water per day!  By the year 2000, this rate increased to 408 billion gallons per day.  On a per person basis in 1990, the U.S. average daily water usage in 1990 was 1,620 gallons, or 1,620 gpcd (gallons per capita per day).  This value actually decreased in 2000 to 1,430 gpcd even though close to 33 million more people lived in the US according to the U.S. Geological Survey (USGS) (2005).

Think about it-if an average bathtub holds 125 gallons, the daily U.S. water usage will fill over 2.7 billion bathtubs, enough for 1 FULL bathtub for half the world’s population-EACH DAY.

Where does all this water come from? A USGS report (2005) states that approximately 85% of the total water used (345 bgd) is freshwater of which 79% comes from surface water sources (rivers, lakes and reservoirs).  Where does all the water go?  In 2000, Texas and California were responsible for over 18% of the total freshwater consumption at 63.2 bgd.  Two additional states, Colorado and Nebraska had water consumption rates greater than 12.5 bgd.  These four states were responsible for over one-quarter of the total daily freshwater consumption in the U.S.  If you are like most people (even if you live in California or Texas), it is highly unlikely that you actually use 1,430 gallons of water each day for bathing, drinking, cooking and/or brushing your teeth.  In fact, most of the water is used in industrial or commercial applications. 

USGS, 2005

In 2000, steam-generating power plants were responsible for approximately 39% of the freshwater daily consumption.  This was primarily used for cooling water purposes.  Due to their high water demand, power plants have historically been located near large bodies of water (the Great Lakes as well as large rivers and oceans).  Cropland irrigation represents 39% of the usage.  Large-scale irrigation is concentrated in the arid Western states.  Approximately 14% of the daily water consumption is used for public water service.  This includes water deliveries to domestic, industrial, commercial and other public purposes (hospitals, pools, parks and public buildings).  Domestic service includes both indoor and outdoor household applications such as drinking and cooking, bathing, washing clothes, flushing toilets and watering lawns and gardens.  Over 60% of public water supplies come from surface water sources.  Some industries do not rely on public or municipal water service to meet their water needs.  Instead, they supply their own water either from wells or surface water sources.  This independent industrial water consumption represented 5% of the total daily use.  Mining, aquaculture and livestock water consumption represents less than 3% of the total daily use.

Water and Industry

Industry uses a large quantity of water in manufacturing their commercial products.  As previously mentioned, this water can be supplied from public/municipal water supplies or independent supplies.  Industrial water requirements vary widely.  For example, approximately 106,000 gallons are used in the production of a single automobile, 2,800 gallons are needed to produce 1 kg (2.2 pounds) of aluminum, 1,300 gallons to produce 1 kg of rice, 230 gallons to produce 1 kg of paper and 60 gallons are needed for 1 kg of steel (Miller, 2004).
