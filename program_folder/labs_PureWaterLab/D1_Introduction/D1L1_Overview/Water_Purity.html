Water Purity Overview

The term “Pure Water” is relative because what might be “pure” enough for us to drink probably won’t be pure enough to be used in a hospital or in industry.  In reality, water is never purely H2O.  Water has the ability to dissolve many substances and often has several different types of contaminants.

Water is termed a "Universal Solvent" because it will disolve virtually everything to which it is exposed.

For uses other than drinking, water must be purified even further.  In labs, it is common to use deionized (DI) water or distilled water.  DI water contains no dissolved anions or cations, which means it has been purified from all ions other than H3O+ and OH-.  DI water may still contain other non-ionic types of impurities such as organic compounds, and it is produced using an ion exchange process.  Distilled water has had virtually all of its impurities removed through distillation.  This is done by boiling the water then collecting the steam as it condenses.  This method is useful in killing off bacteria, but it does not remove any larger contaminants such as sand.  Each method is good, but they work best in combination with other methods. 

Pharmaceutical grade water must be pure but not pure enough to endanger those who will take the drugs.  There are two basic breakdowns of pharmaceutical grade water:    Water for Injection (WFI) and Purified Water (PW).  The regulations are basically the same; however, the bacteria content for WFI must be lower.  Municipal drinking water must have its ion content lowered to reduce its conductivity.  The water must then be run through a membrane filter to remove total organic carbon.  Finally, the bacteria are managed by running through UV light.

Ultrapure water (UPW) is a type of highly purified water. Almost all of its impurities have been removed and it is virtually theoretically pure. To give an example, it is like the water in a 50 meters swimming pool containing a single grain of salt. The water we drink every day is equivalent to the same volume of water containing as much as 200 kg (441 lb) of salt. The purity of the UPW is more than several orders of magnitude greater than that of drinking water.  The contaminants found in UPW are generally measured in parts per billion (ppb) because they are so dilute.
